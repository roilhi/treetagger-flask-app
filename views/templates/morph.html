A tool for derivational suffixes identification, counts and tagging

Morph 1.0 is a rule and corpus-based Natural Language Processing (NLP) web application designed to identify derivational suffixes and morphologically complex words in English texts. The tool is intended for corpus-based research, with particular application to learner corpora and the study of morphological productivity.

Development of Morph

The development of Morph includes the next steps:
1. Suffix Compilation and Verification

A list of 90 derivational suffixes was compiled and verified using established morphological reference works, including Bauer, Lieber & Plag (2007); Stein (2007); Laws & Ryder (2014) and the Oxford English Dictionary (2026).Suffix descriptions were verified in terms of morphological function, word-class distribution, form and allomorphic variation, semantic contribution, orthographic alterations. Only suffixes with verified morphological status were included.

2. Alignment of Existing Morphological Databases

Complex word candidates were identified through structured alignment of entries from MorphoQuantics (Laws & Ryder, 2026) and MorphoLex (Sánchez-Gutiérrez, Mailhot, Deacon & Williams, 2018). Suffix inventories from these databases were aligned to the verified 90-suffix framework.

3. Manual Verification and Etymological Control

Where discrepancies or mismatches in morphological structure or etymology were identified, entries were manually verified using the Oxford English Dictionary. This process ensured:

  • Accurate derivational segmentation
  • Correct base identification
  • Exclusion of opaque or non-derivational forms

4. Lemma Standardisation and Inflectional Expansion

Verified complex forms were standardised as lemmas. Inflectional variants were added following verification through the Oxford English Dictionary and the British National Corpus (BNC) evidence. The resulting database contains approximately 27,791 complex word entries aligned to 90 derivational suffixes.

Intended Research Applications

Morph is designed to support:
  • Morphological productivity studies
  • Learner corpus research
  • Interlanguage morphology analysis
  • Creative derivation studies
  • Type/token morphological profiling
  • Corpus-assisted language research
