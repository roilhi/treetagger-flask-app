Mexican Learner Corpus 3.0

An open, longitudinal learner corpus project supporting rigorous, community-oriented research on second language development.

Mexican Learner Corpus (MexLeC) is an independent, nationwide, longitudinal research project, conceived and coordinated by Ana Abigahil Flores Hernández, who holds authorship of the original concept, methodology and corpus design. The data collected form part of an open-access repository for the academic community, conceived as a common good rather than the exclusive property of any individual or institution. Collaborating institutions facilitate access to students and educational contexts. Subsequent applications and research derived from these data may be conducted collaboratively or independently. MexLeC is a spoken, longitudinal corpus of Mexican learners of second languages. Its main aim is to represent the spoken production of University students enrolled in BA programmes in Mexican universities with emphasis on second languages (linguistics, translation and teaching), tracking their production throughout their three to four years programmes.

Data collection instrument

The instrument for collection is an interview divided into three sections/tasks representing two productive activities according to the CEFR Companion Volume (Council of Europe, 2020): sustained monologue describing experiences and sustained monologue putting a case. As well as three conversation text types according to Biber (2004): information-focused, stance-focused and narrative-focused interpreted in linguistic and functional terms.

A sustained monologue describing experience concerns narrative and description, including aspects describing form simple everyday information such as description of oneself, occupation and place of living. Including functions like: describing plans and arrangements, habits and routines, past activities and personal experiences. Finally this productive activity includes subjects related to fields of interest and detailed descriptions on complex subjects. Regarding the complexity of discourse, this is described as including simple words/signs, formulaic expressions, simple sentences, short paragraphs, sequence of points integrating sub-themes and developing particular points in a flowing description (Council of Europe, 2020).

A sustained monologue putting a case describes the ability to sustain an argument, this is the ability to express what one likes or dislikes, opinions on subjects relating to everyday life as well as topical and complex issues. Additionally this communicative activity includes the ability to make simple comparisons, expanding and supporting viewpoints and developing arguments also taking into account others’ perspectives and emphasizing. Finally, this communicative activity includes the presentation of ideas, highlighting of significant points and the formulation of well-structured precise points.

Information-focused text types are related to informational purposes and are associated with elaborated noun phrases and a dense integration of information in a text. Main linguistics features in this text type are word length, nominalisations, prepositional phrases, abstract nouns, relative clauses, attributive adjectives, passive verb phrases (likelihood adverbs and general hedges).

Stance-focused text types relate to personal attitudes or likelihood. Linguistic features associated with this text type are that-deletions, mental verbs, factual/mental verbs + that-clause, likelihood/mental verb + that-clause, likelihood adverbs, adverbial clauses, general hedges, factual adverbs.

Narrative-focused text types relate to structured accounts of connected events either experiences or stories (real or imagined). Features in this textual type include past tense verbs, 3rd person pronouns, non-factual/communication verb + that-clause, communication verbs, that-deletions.

The procedure for collection consists of four stages:

  1. Registration of volunteer participants from different universities in second semester at any public university including the signing of an informed consent form to record and use data for research purposes;
  2. Remote interview application and video-recording;
  3. Semiautomatic transcription of the interview following MexLeC guidelines;
  4. Uploading of the final versions of anonymised scripts and audio files organised by university and interview section.

MexLeC previous stages

Stages of this project include Version 1.0, the pilot study described in Flores and Moore (2024), and Version 2.0 of the collection, also described in Flores and Moore (2024), available here. Both stages included data collection from 2020 to 2025, with a total of approximately 50 participants from 11 universities across Mexico. The data collected in these stages were transcribed and formatted according to the conventions described in the next section. Both stages were financed by the Secretaría de Ciencia, Humanidades, Tecnología e Innovación (SECIHTI) through a personal postdoctoral internship (CVU 482854) at the Facultad de Lenguas of the Universidad Autónoma del Estado de México from October 1st, 2020 to November 30th, 2025.

Organising previous data into the new 3.0 scheme

For the new schema, new interviews (2026) were conducted and previous interviews (2020–2025) were aligned following the same three discourse criteria. Describing experiences sustained monologue and information-focused text type, tagged as DI (Part 1 of the interview); describing experiences sustained monologue and narrative-focused text type, tagged as DN (Part 2 of the interview); and putting-a-case (argumentative) sustained monologue and stance-focused text type, tagged as AS (Part 3 of the interview). To align previous interviews to this schema, when these sections were found in the interview video (and transcriptions), they were separated, aligned and coded accordingly. This process involved the elimination of Universidad Autónoma del Estado de México full section and Universidad Autónoma del Estado de Querétaro and Universidad del Estado de Hidalgo first cohorts. Participants were also aligned for each university according to the term (semester) and level of proficiency taken from the learning materials the participants use and the levels described in their programmes instead of separating data by cohort. Finally, transcriptions were revised and manually corrected according to the 3.0 conventions as follows:

MexLeC 3.0 transcription and formatting conventions

General formatting conventions

Font: Arial, 12-point size.
Alignment: Left-aligned.
Language variety: British English.
Punctuation: No punctuation marks are used. The only exception is the use of ¿ ? to indicate interrogative intonation.

Research participants information

All personal or identifying information is anonymised. Personal names, addresses, and places are systematically replaced with the tags <name> <address> and <place>.

Linguistic information

Capitalisation follows standard English conventions, including proper names, countries, languages, nationalities, days of the week, and months.
Capital letters are also used when participants explicitly spell letters. Example: I’d like to visit USA
Numerals are not used. All numbers are written out in words. Example: She was born in nineteen eighty.
Formal and informal contractions are transcribed exactly as produced by the speaker. Examples: don’t, it’s, wanna
Interjections are enclosed in angle brackets and are limited to the following set: <er>, <uh-uh>, <uh-huh>, <huh>, <oh>, <wow>, <ugh>, <phew>, <meh>.
False starts and self-repairs are marked with an equals sign (=) at the end of the interrupted word or phrase.
Foreign-language material is marked using the following format: < foreign=... >.

Extralinguistic information

Inaudible speech is marked as follows: <x> for a single phoneme, <xx> for an entire word and, <xxx> for a longer stretch of speech.
Speaker turns are indicated by a page break.
Pauses are marked using angle brackets: <.> short pause (up to three seconds) and, <..> long pause (more than three seconds).
Non-linguistic sounds and events may be transcribed using descriptive tags, including but not limited to: <laugh>, <clear throat>, <sigh>, <alveolar click>.

Ethical statement

All participants provided informed consent prior to participation. Consent included permission to record, transcribe, anonymise and use the data for research and academic dissemination purposes. All personal and identifying information has been anonymised following established corpus linguistics practices. The project complies with applicable Mexican data protection regulations. Audio files containing potentially identifiable information may be subject to controlled access. MexLeC is an open resource. Users are requested to complete a brief form using an institutional email account to document and track research projects conducted with the corpus.

Versionising policy

  • Version (2024) 1.0 - Pilot
  • Version 2.0 (2025) - Expanded sample
  • Version 3.0 (2026) - Longitudinal alignment and discourse reclassification and expanded sample.

Access conditions

Transcriptions and audio files fully open for research purposes. Mp4 files upon request for research purposes.
